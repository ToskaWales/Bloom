Why your strength peaks mid-cycle
Estrogen doesn't just regulate your period — it affects your muscles, joints, and pain tolerance.
Hormones

During your follicular and ovulatory phases, estrogen surges. This hormone binds to receptors in muscle tissue, improving protein synthesis and reducing muscle damage from exercise. Studies show strength can be up to 15% higher near ovulation. This is your power window — Bloom will always suggest heavier training here.

Why rest is productive training
Your luteal phase is for consolidation, not punishment. Here's the science.
Training

During your luteal phase, progesterone rises and body temperature increases slightly. Recovery takes longer and your body uses more carbohydrate at rest. Training through fatigue here raises cortisol and impairs the gains you made earlier. Rest in the luteal phase is what lets your follicular phase be powerful. Bloom tracks this for you.

🥑
Eating with your cycle for energy
What you eat at each phase can dramatically change how you feel and perform.
Nutrition

Menstrual: Iron-rich foods (leafy greens, lentils) to replace what's lost. Anti-inflammatory foods like berries and omega-3s ease cramps.

Follicular: Light, energising foods. Your digestion is efficient here — great time for higher carb fuel.

Ovulatory: High protein to support muscle building. Zinc and B vitamins.

Luteal: Magnesium-rich foods reduce PMS (dark chocolate, pumpkin seeds 🎉). Complex carbs help with cravings.

🌙
Sleep & your cycle
Progesterone affects your deep sleep. Here's why you feel more tired in your luteal phase.
Recovery

Progesterone has a sedative effect, which can make you feel drowsy in the luteal phase but also disrupts deep sleep quality. Your core temperature rises slightly, making deep sleep harder. This is why quality rest matters more than total hours. Bloom's luteal workouts are scheduled earlier in the day to protect your sleep.

🌊
Understanding PMS vs. normal luteal symptoms
Not everything you feel in week 3-4 is PMS. Your body is doing important work.
Hormones

The luteal phase involves a rise and then a sharp fall of both estrogen and progesterone. The fall triggers the uterine lining to shed. Mood shifts, bloating, and fatigue are common and normal physiological responses — not weakness. Bloom never pathologises these experiences. Instead, it uses them as signals to reduce intensity and prioritise recovery.
